08 · Synthesis
Key Findings & Implications
Eight evidence-based conclusions from the cleaned dataset, intended for review by administrators, researchers, and peers.

Finding 1 — Industrial ≠ Dominant (After Cleaning)

The raw data's apparent Industrial Pharmacy lead was an artifact of repeat submissions. Cleaned data shows a genuine dead heat between Industrial and Community Pharmacy (45 students each). Career counselors should treat both as equally prioritised by the class.

Finding 2 — Community Pharmacy Offers the Surest Footing

Community Pharmacy students have the highest mean confidence (86.4%), the highest rate of full certainty (56.8%), and the lowest rate of "Only God knows" (1 student). The self-employment route via community pharmacy provides a visible, tangible career pathway.

Finding 3 — Academia is Haemorrhaging Confidence

Among primary Academia/Research students, the mean practice confidence is just 56.3% — 30 points below Community Pharmacy — and zero are fully confident. Nigerian pharmacy academia urgently needs better career visibility: fellowships, postdoc pathways, faculty recruitment pipelines.

Finding 4 — Admin/Regulatory: A Hidden Confidence Crisis

Four of five primary Administrative/Regulatory students gave "Only God knows" rather than a percentage. This 80% spiritual-deferral rate — the highest of any domain — suggests these students see the fewest clear pathways. Regulatory bodies like NAFDAC may need more robust internship and entry-level visibility programmes.

Finding 5 — Multi-Domain Students Are More Confident

Students with multiple specialisation interests average 85.9% practice confidence vs. 79.3% for single-domain students — a 6.6 point gap. Career flexibility may reduce anxiety about uncertain job markets. Pharmacy curricula might benefit from exposing students to broader domain possibilities.

Finding 6 — 300L and 400L Are Both Crucibles

After deduplication, the trauma gap between 400L (45.7%) and 300L (42.2%) is just 3.5 points — far narrower than raw data implied. The entire 300L–400L stretch is the program's psychological pressure chamber. Pastoral care interventions should span both years, not just final year.

Finding 7 — Law is the Most-Wanted Alternative Career

Law (7 mentions) is the #1 alternative career in the cleaned data — significantly ahead of Medicine and Computer Science. The overlap in analytical/advisory skills between pharmacy and law is notable. Some students may be drawn by perceived earnings or courtroom prestige.

Finding 8 — 89.2% Participation is an Asset

With 124 unique named respondents from a 139-student class, the survey achieved near-complete coverage. This makes the findings broadly generalisable to the entire cohort — not just the vocal minority — giving administrators reliable baseline data for career planning decisions.

09 · Methodology
Data Cleaning & Methodology Notes
Full transparency on decisions made during cleaning — critical for downstream review.

Deduplication Strategy

Names were normalised by stripping whitespace, collapsing multiple spaces, and converting to lowercase before matching. For each duplicate name group, the most recent submission (by Timestamp) was retained. This preserves a student's latest, potentially most considered response.

Anonymous Submissions

41 submissions had no name (NaN in the Full Name field). These are completely excluded from all analyses since they cannot be deduplicated, linked to a student, or verified as unique. They may represent test submissions, non-student completions, or anonymised intents.

Specialisation Categorisation

Free-text responses were mapped to canonical domains using keyword matching. Variations like "Industry", "Industrial pharmacy", "INDUSTRIAL PHARMACY" were all mapped to Industrial Pharmacy. Where a response contained keywords from multiple domains, the student was counted in each.

Confidence Mean Calculation

"Only God knows" (17 students) and blank responses were excluded from numeric mean calculations. All average confidence figures are computed on the 107-student numeric subset. This is noted explicitly wherever means are cited.

Survey Period

Submissions span November 19, 2025 to April 16, 2026 — a 5-month collection window suggesting the form remained open well past its initial distribution. This explains why some students submitted multiple times across different months.

Class Size Assumption

The participation rate of 89.2% uses the user-stated class size of ~139 students. If the actual graduating class size differs, the participation rate should be recalculated accordingly. The 124 unique respondent count is precise; the denominator is an approximation.
